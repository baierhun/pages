# **Class Debate: "Resolved: Machines Can Be Intelligent"**

In this debate, you will explore the question: Can machines truly be intelligent? You will work in teams to research examples, develop arguments, and respond to opposing points. Some examples may support the idea that machines can be intelligent, while others highlight their limitations. Your goal is to use evidence and reasoning to persuade the class of your team’s position. Each team member must be their team's speaker for one round.

This debate will help you practice critical thinking, public speaking, and evaluating complex ideas in technology and ethics.

## **Roles**

1. **Team Affirmative (4 students)** – Argues **in favor** of the resolution.
2. **Team Negative (4 students)** – Argues **against** the resolution.
3. **Moderators (2 students)** – Facilitate the debate, keep time, and manage the Q&A.

---

## **Debate Structure (50 minutes)**

### **1. Introduction & Setup (5 minutes)**

* Moderators explain the rules and time limits.
* Teams announce their speaker order.
* Each moderator takes note of speaking times and who will ask questions during rebuttals.

---

### **2. Opening Statements (8 minutes)**

* **Affirmative Team:** 4 minutes
* **Negative Team:** 4 minutes

**Instructions:**
* Each team presents their main argument.
* Focus on 2–3 key points.
* No interruptions from the opposing team.

---

### **3. Rebuttal Round (8 minutes)**

* **Affirmative Team:** 4 minutes
* **Negative Team:** 4 minutes

**Instructions:**
* Respond directly to the opposing team’s opening statements.
* Provide counterarguments and evidence.
* Speak in turn (e.g., one speaker per team).

---

### **4. Cross-Examination / Q&A (10 minutes)**

* Moderators take turns asking prepared or spontaneous questions to each team (5 minutes each).

**Instructions for Teams:**
* Answer clearly and concisely.
* You may refer back to your points and discuss how to respond as a team but avoid repeating your entire speech.

**Instructions for Moderators:**
* Ask 3-5 questions per team as time allows.
* Ensure all team members have a chance to answer.
* Keep each response under 1 minute.
* No one should know the questions in advance.

---

### **5. Closing Statements (6 minutes)**

* **Affirmative Team:** 3 minutes
* **Negative Team:** 3 minutes

**Instructions:**
* Summarize your strongest arguments.
* Rebut the most critical points from the other team.
* Leave a lasting impression.

---

### **6. Audience / Class Vote & Reflection (3 minutes)**

* If there is an audience, they can vote on which team presented the strongest arguments.
* Quick reflection on what arguments were persuasive and how each team handled counterpoints.

---

## **Time Management Overview**

| Segment                 | Duration  |
| ----------------------- | --------- |
| Setup & instructions    | 5 min     |
| Opening statements      | 8 min     |
| Rebuttals               | 8 min     |
| Cross-Examination / Q&A | 10 min    |
| Closing statements      | 6 min     |
| Reflection & wrap-up    | 3 min     |
| **Total**               | 40–50 min |

---

## **Tips for Each Role**

**Team Members:**

* Plan who speaks when.
* Use examples, definitions, and logic to support points.
* Listen carefully to the other team’s arguments for rebuttals.

**Moderators:**

* Keep track of time.
* Ensure all rules are followed.
* Clarify any confusion about speaking order or time limits.

**Everyone:**

* Listen carefully: Pay attention to the other team to prepare strong rebuttals.
* Speak clearly: Use confident tone and clear reasoning.
* Use examples: Real-world or hypothetical examples strengthen your points.
* Stay on time: Each section has a strict time limit—moderators will help.
